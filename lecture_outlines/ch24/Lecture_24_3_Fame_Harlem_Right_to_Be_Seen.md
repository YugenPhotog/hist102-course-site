# Lecture 3 — Fame, Harlem, and the Right to Be Seen

## Lecture Focus
This lecture connects movies, photography, and the Harlem Renaissance as struggles over image control. The 1920s become a contest over who gets to be visible, admired, and remembered.

## Big Questions
- How did movies and photography reshape American identity?
- Why was visibility itself a form of power in the 1920s?
- How did Black artists use images to challenge racist representations?

## I. Movies and the Invention of Celebrity
- Movie palaces create shared national culture.
- Silent film acting emphasizes exaggerated emotion and gesture.
- Studio publicity photographs standardize beauty and desire.
- Celebrities become moral and cultural reference points.

## II. Learning How to Be Seen
- Photography teaches posing, self-presentation, and aspiration.
- Faces become commodities.
- Fame becomes reproducible and scalable.
- Americans learn to imagine themselves as visible subjects.

## III. Harlem as Cultural Capital
- Harlem emerges as a center of Black artistic production.
- Writers, musicians, and photographers collaborate implicitly.
- Art expresses joy, pride, frustration, and limitation.
- Harlem is both a real community and a national symbol.

## IV. Photography as Self-Definition
- Studio portraits emphasize dignity and elegance.
- Black families commission images that counter racist stereotypes.
- Photography becomes a form of historical self-authorship.
- Respectability is not submission but strategy.

## V. Limits of Visibility
- Cultural recognition does not equal political equality.
- Harlem Renaissance flourishes within segregation.
- Images circulate beyond the control of their creators.
- Visibility can empower and constrain at the same time.

## Closing Takeaway
In the 1920s, being seen mattered. Movies and photography turned visibility into power, and the Harlem Renaissance used that power creatively — even as its limits remained painfully clear.
