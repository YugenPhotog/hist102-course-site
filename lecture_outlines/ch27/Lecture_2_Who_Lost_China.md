
# Lecture 2 — “Who Lost China?” Fear, Expertise, and the Birth of the Security State

## Lecture Purpose
This lecture traces how the Chinese Communist victory became a domestic political trauma that reshaped American governance, delegitimized expertise, and accelerated the creation of a permanent national security state.

## Key Questions
- Why did the fall of China produce panic rather than reflection?
- How did foreign policy failure become a domestic loyalty issue?
- Why did ambiguity become politically dangerous?

## I. The Shock of Revolution
- Communist victory in China
- Psychological impact on American policymakers
- Perception of global momentum toward communism

## II. “Who Lost China?”
- Framing failure as betrayal
- Moral accusation versus strategic analysis
- Political usefulness of blame

## III. The China Lobby
- Anti-communist activism
- Pressure on Congress and the executive branch
- Simplification of complex foreign realities

## IV. Attacks on Expertise
- Targeting the China Hands
- Suspicion of diplomats and scholars
- Replacement of analysis with ideological certainty

## V. Containment as Worldview
- Truman Doctrine
- Binary moral framework: free vs. unfree
- End of policy flexibility

## VI. Birth of the National Security State
- National Security Act of 1947
- Creation of CIA, NSC, Department of Defense
- Loyalty programs and ideological screening

## Takeaway
Fear of humiliation and loss—not just fear of communism—drives the construction of permanent emergency governance.
