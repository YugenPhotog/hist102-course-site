# Lecture 2 — Civil Rights as a Global Legitimacy Crisis  
**Chapter 28: America at Midcentury, 1953–1963**

## Central Question
Why did civil rights become a national priority only when it became an international problem?

## Framing Argument
Civil rights in the 1950s was not only a moral struggle—it was a geopolitical liability.

---

## I. Segregation and Cold War Optics
- U.S. claims leadership of the free world
- Racial inequality undermines credibility
- Soviet propaganda exploits American racism

## II. Brown v. Board and Its Limits
- Legal victory without social enforcement
- Federal hesitation reveals limits of courts

## III. Grassroots Escalation
- Boycotts, protests, mass participation
- Shift from legal to direct action

## IV. Beyond the South
- Northern segregation and housing
- Native American policy conflicts
- Mexican American activism
- Urban–suburban inequality

## V. Civil Rights and Credibility
- Democracy must be demonstrated, not claimed
- Cold War pressure accelerates reform

## Concluding Takeaway
Civil rights advanced because injustice became internationally unsustainable.
